Charles Babbage

Inventor of the Very First Computer


(1791 - 1871)

A tool is usually more simple than a machine; it is generally used with the hand, whilst a machine is frequently moved by animal or steam power.

At each increase of knowledge, as well as on the contrivance of every new tool, human labour becomes abridged.

Charles Babbage was born on Dec. 26, 1791 in England. He was one of four children born to Betsy Plumleigh Teape and Benjamin Babbage. His father was a banker.
Charles moved around for his education as he was ill as a child due to fever. He would end up being sent to the countryside, and other schools along with having a private tutor at home. He eventually ended up at Holmwood academy in Enfield, Middlesex. This is where his love of mathematics started.
1810 - 1814: He attended Trinity College in Cambridge and moved to Peterhouse College, Cambridge in 1812. He was the top mathematician. He graduated in 1814.
Jobs
Babbage became a lecturer at the Royal Institution where he lectured about astronomy and in 1816, he was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society.
1820: Babbage helped found the Astronomical Society and in 1824 he won its Gold Medal, for his invention of an engine for calculating mathematical and astronomical tables.
1828 to 1839: Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge University.
Machines
1819 - 1822: Babbage built his first small model of the calculating engine (Difference Engine 0). Powered by cranking a handle, the machine calculated and printed mathematical tables. The machine was called a difference engine after the mathematical theory on which the machine's operation was based.
Difference Engine No.1 was designed to calculate and tabulate polynomial functions. Babbage was given Â£1,700 by the British Government to begin a full scale machine (Difference Engine No. 1). Over the years more than 10 times this amount of money was spent on the project. In 1832, a small working portion was built. In 1833, work on the full scale difference engine was stopped. If the machine had been built it would have had over 25,000 working parts, weighted over 13 metric tons and have been over 8 feet (2.4m) tall.
In 1837, Babbage became interested in building a more ambitious analytical engine that would be more powerful than the original difference engine. This would be the first working computer for general-purpose computation. It would use punched cards for programming. A trial part was built but was never fully completed. He also designed a printer for his new engine.
The government decided to stop funding the projects in 1842.
1846 - 1849: Babbage designed a new improved difference engine (Difference Engine No. 2).
These machines were considered the very first mechanical computers to be invented. The fact that they were not actually built was due to a lack of funding and not due to any design flaw. The basic concept of his machines was similar to the modern computer which is why he is known as the father of the computer.
Publications and Inventions
1832: published On the Economy of Machinery and Manufactures about industrial production which was well received. In the book, it described what is now known as the Babbage Principle which noted the advantages of using division of labor in factories.
1837: wrote a natural theology book On the Power, Wisdom and Goodness of God, as manifested in the Creation in 1837.
1838: Babbage invented the pilot (also called a cow-catcher), which is the metal frame attached to the front of locomotives that clears the tracks of obstacles.
Babbage also invented an ophthalmoscope, which is used in eye examinations.
Personal Life and Death
1814: Babbage married Georgiana Whitmore. They had eight children together, but only three lived beyond childhood. His wife died in 1827.
1871: Charles Babbage died on Oct. 18, 1871, aged 79. He is buried in the Kensal Green Cemetery in London. Cause of death was renal inadequacy.
